# Introduction

Thinking about albums probably conjures thoughts of musical artistry. Indeed, seeing your favorite album cover probably conjures memories of your favorite songs. For me, seeing the album for New Order's *Substance* brings back memories of sleeping in a car listening to *Blue Monday* for many hours. However, this project is not about the art of music; it's about the art of album covers. Keep reading to learn why I decided to laser engrave 25 album covers and display them on my wall.

# The Art of Album Covers

Something that I feel has perhaps been lost with the advent of downloading and streaming music is just how neat album covers can be. While I am certainly not old enough to speak from experience, I imagine album covers were once essential to selling vinyl records and CDs in physical stores. Similar to a candy wrapper, album covers set an expectation for what the musical contents of an album held. Since music tends to vary highly, album art naturally takes wildly different approaches to representing the mood and style of music, resulting in lots of interesting art. Additionally, album art can include symbolism that music can't, further contextualizing the music.

So that I could appreciate the artistry of album art in a novel way, I laser engraved a selection of my favorite albums and displayed them. Of course, you've already seen the final product at the top of this page; each wooden album cover measures 5.25" (the same size as a floppy disk), and was engraved on a Trotec laser. Since engraving on wood is different from conventional printing, a lot of time was dedicated to tweaking the art to look good on wood.

Frankly, there's not much more to explain about the project; I simply enjoy album art and appreciate the nuance associated with it. In case you aren't convinced of the awesomeness of album art, allow me to share some specific examples that may win you over.

# Cool Examples of Album Art

## Unknown Pleasures (Joy Division)

You may have seen this iconic album cover from Joy Division's *Unknown Pleasures*. It may look like just a bunch of wavy lines, but the neat part of the design is that the lines aren't random. In fact, the lines are a recording of the B1919+21 pulsar and were based on a picture from the 1977 edition of The Cambridge Encyclopaedia of Astronomy.

## Soundshock: FM Funk Maddness!! (Ubiktune)

TODO: Include picture of album and FM algo

FM Funk Maddness (and its two sequel albums), while likely not an album you've ever heard of, delivers an idiosyncratic mix of music produced with frequency modulation (FM). The album's cover art mirrors the chaotic nature of the album's name. Additionally, many characters can be seen surfing on chips (one is a YM2151 FM chip). The energy emitted from the chips resembles an FM algorithm (i.e., how FM operators are combined together to produce a sound). Obviously, this is relatively esoteric nerd-knowledge. Nevertheless, it's still neat.
