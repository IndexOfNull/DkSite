# Introduction/Summary

As an avid pin-trader, I have a lot of experience with pin clutches (the part that goes on the back of pins) harming my fingers. After a night of reorganizing my pins, I decided to make a tool to make removing pin backs easier. Over the course of three years, I have designed, manufactured, and refined three tools for this purpose. Keep reading to hear more about why I built this tool and how I've revised it over time.

# The Story

{{< figure src="/images/projects/pin-popper/pincollection.jpg" caption="The pin collection in question (I have a lot more now)" >}}

As I've mentioned, I am mildly obsessed with pin trading. Specifically, I trade pins for Odyssey of the Mind, a sort of engineering/performing arts competition that I volunteer for. Naturally, that meant I had to deal with a lot of the little rubber pin backs securing pins in place. Normally, removing these involves pinching them with your fingers and pulling up, which rapidly grows tiring. I eventually had enough and began brainstorming tool to make the process easier.

My mind immediately jumped to keycap removers. In principle, keycap removers solve a similar problem to mine: gripping and removing something. With the design of a keycap puller in mind, I twisted a few paper clips together to make possibly the world's most flimsy prototype ever (I regrettably do not have a picture of it). While the prototype was questionable, the design showed promise.

Having recently learned Fusion 360, I figured it would be a perfect opportunity to test out my new skills. With the clock ticking past midnight, I got to work and made another prototype:

{{< figure src="/images/projects/pin-popper/firstdesign.jpg" caption="The next iteration" >}}

I 3D printed this design and found that it was almost as flimsy as the paper clips. It bent too much and was too unweildy to be used effectively. At this point, I realized ergonomics and the physical properties of plastic would have a major influence in my design. Around this time, I also began giving serious thought to making large quantities of my tool to distribute; manufacturing time and cost became an important factor as well. From there, the prototyping cycle continued. I tweaked the thickness and stiffness of the tool, shortened it to make it easier to hold, and added a few other creature comforts until I was happy.

# From Design to Practice

With a design I was happy with, I turned my attention to mass-manufacturing. I ran my school's 3D printer for 20 hours churning out pin poppers. I managed to use a little over a kilogram of filament in the process, yielding over 100 pin poppers.

{{< figure src="/images/projects/pin-popper/batchofpullers.png" caption="One batch (of three)" >}}

Everything finished printing *just* before I had to load up and head off for the event. The timing was so tight I wound up post-processing the prints during the drive up.

## The Public Response

Other people wound up really liking my idea. People would excitedly show off my little pair of tweezers to their friends, who'd usually ask for a pair for themselves (good thing I made a lot). A pin trader helped distribute them for me; it was always fun to randomly see someone using my tool. Some of the tools wound up in people with dexterity issues; it was great to hear how it made pin trading possible for some people. Also, while I gave most pin poppers away for free, they turned out to be a good item to pin trade with, though I mostly just threw them is as an extra bonus.

# Version Two (2023)

{{< figure src="/images/projects/pin-popper/versiontwo.jpg" caption="Quite similar to the original design, but requires less accuracy and is more comfortable to use." >}}

I have a strong tendency to outdo myself, so one year later (2023), I went back to iterate on the design. The specifics of the design changes aren't important. I continued improving upon the ergonomics and usability of the design. I went through a similar manufacturing process as above, though with different filament and a different printer (a decision that I would ultimately regret). Of course, another year brought another Odyssey of the Mind world finals and a chance to share an improved design.

The story went much like before; people continued to love the design. Two things were slightly different this time, though.

First, the new tools were highly susceptible to breaking. In my naiveté, I chose to use silk filament because it looked cool, not knowing it was significantly weaker than regular PLA filament. It was quite embarrassing that the supposedly improved design was more vulnerable to breaking. After some experimentation, I discovered that twisting the tool in a certain way would cause the finger rests on the arms to break. Needless to say, seeing my creation practically fail did not make me too happy, but I duly noted the failure for future revisions.

Second, my first design had been copied. This had initially frustrated me, but I soon came to terms with the fact that people liked the idea, so it being replicated was inevitable. As it turned out, the person who copied the design was in middle school and replicated the tool near dimensionally-accurate perfection—better than I would have done in middle school. He also fixed a difficult to spot issue I had made in the original design. Overall, I'm happy my idea was good enough to copy.

# Version Three (2024)

{{< figure src="/images/projects/pin-popper/versionthree.png" >}}

For my third revision, I decided to completely rethink the design of the tool, focusing on the aspects that I found most important: durability, cost (i.e., maximizing yield with as little material), and portability. This led me to create a design that is similar to the claw end of a hammer. Since the design had more of a prying action, I renamed the tool to "PinPry".

The design process for the third version was far more involved, requiring about eight revisions to ensure good ergonomics. The initial redesign fit my design criteria almost perfectly, but upon testing the design with people, it became clear that it was not intuitive to use.

{{< figure src="/images/projects/pin-popper/versionthree-early.png" caption="How would you grip this?" >}}

Gradually, I revised the design to accomodate intuition, ultimately landing on a design that is reminiscent of a fork.

Like before, I manufactured the tools with a 3D printer. Since 3D printing produces anisotropic parts, special attention was paid to orienting the parts for manufacturing. Each tool was printed with the thin side adhering to the bed: an orientation normally terrible for bed adhesion. However, sacrificing bed adhesion was essential, as it allowed parts to be strongest on the axis that would see the most load. Naturally, this slightly decreased yield. However, one kilogram of filament still produced approximately 200 tools (failures excluded)—nearly twice that of the first two designs.

Once more, I went to Odyssey of the Mind World Finals to distribute this design. Most people agreed that this design was superior to the previous ones in terms of portability and usability, but a select few prefered the older design. Indeed, I do think the older designs have justifiable reason to exist. Notably, I imagine this newer design is not as well suited for those with dexterity limitations.

---

# Conclusion

What started as a simple project to make my life easier evolved into an endeavor involving manufacturing, multiple revisions, and designing around peoples' intuition. Moreover, while pin trading is by no means an important activity, I'm happy to put my little contribution out into the world. It was a fun time learning, if nothing else.

As I write this, I can't think of what a version four would look like. I think the third version is the best it can be, eliminating the need for another revision. Then again, I said that after version two, so I suppose we'll just have to wait and see...
